The Problem

Weâ€™ve Lost Touch with Our Roots

Industrialization led many families to leave rural areas for urban centers around 250 years ago, diminishing our connection to agriculture. With only a small fraction of jobs now in farming, generations have become detached from the knowledge of food production and seasonal eating. Ignorance about food sources is evident, with surveys revealing startling misconceptions even among children. Lack of compulsory cooking education exacerbates the issue, leaving many unaware of how to prepare healthy meals.

The Power of the Big Supermarkets

Reliance on supermarkets for food shopping has increased dramatically, with four major chains dominating the market. While supermarkets offer convenience and affordability, their dominance poses several challenges. Independent local businesses struggle to compete, leading to the decline of high streets and the proliferation of chain stores. Small-scale farmers face pressure from supermarkets demanding lower prices, driving many out of business. Concentration of market power has prompted calls for regulatory oversight, resulting in the establishment of measures to ensure fair treatment of suppliers.

Putting All Our Eggs in Four Baskets

The consolidation of retail giants has also streamlined the supply chain, increasing vulnerability to disruptions. Fewer suppliers are tasked with meeting the demands of supermarkets, creating longer and more complex supply chains. This heightens the risk of contamination and supply chain disruptions, as seen in incidents like the 2013 horsemeat scandal. Reliance on cheap oil for transportation further compounds these risks, leaving the food system susceptible to price fluctuations and logistical challenges.

What We Can Do

Grow Our Own

A resurgence in home gardening and community food growing initiatives offers a solution. Community gardens and allotments provide opportunities to reconnect with food production, promote organic practices, and enhance urban environments. Beyond improving access to fresh produce, community gardening fosters social cohesion, skill-building, and physical well-being.

Spread Around Our Shopping Pounds

Supporting local businesses keeps money circulating within communities, sustains local economies, and fosters social connections. Shopping locally reduces reliance on cars, promotes healthier lifestyles, and preserves the character of neighborhoods. Various resources and organizations facilitate access to local food outlets, farmers' markets, and community-supported agriculture schemes.

Ask Businesses and Policy Makers to Take Action

Local authorities play a crucial role in supporting local food systems. Policies that prioritize community food growing, encourage local food businesses, and promote sustainable practices can strengthen local economies and enhance food security. Advocacy efforts aimed at policymakers can drive positive change and create a more resilient and equitable food system.
