The problem

Empty Seas

Our insatiable appetite for fish has surged dramatically over the past 50 years, exacerbating the overexploitation of nearly 90% of the world’s fisheries. This alarming trend not only imperils marine ecosystems but also jeopardizes the livelihoods of millions of people reliant on fishing for their sustenance.

Issues associated with fishing include:

  • The inadvertent capture of endangered species and juvenile fish as bycatch
  • The wasteful discarding of unwanted catch, contributing to the depletion of fish populations
  • The degradation and loss of critical marine habitats, disrupting entire ecosystems
  • The pervasive problem of illegal, unreported, and unregulated fishing practices

The Common Fisheries Policy (CFP) governs fishing practices within the European Union but may lack the necessary rigor to curb the damage inflicted on marine environments. The compounding issue of illegal fishing exacerbates the problem, particularly in regions crucial for the food security of impoverished communities.

Fish farming, often heralded as a solution, presents its own set of challenges such as pollution, disease outbreaks, and the degradation of natural habitats.

Fish and Health

While fish, especially oily varieties, offer undeniable health benefits, their consumption must be balanced with environmental considerations. Pollutants such as mercury and PCBs pose significant health risks, particularly to vulnerable populations like pregnant women.

What We Can Do

  • Make conscientious choices by avoiding purchasing fish from overexploited stocks or poorly managed fisheries. Utilize resources like the Marine Conservation Society’s 'fish to avoid' list for guidance.

  • Explore Sustainable Fish City’s recommendations for sustainable alternatives to popular fish species, ensuring your consumption aligns with environmental stewardship.

  • Throw your support behind campaigns advocating for sustainable fishing practices and marine conservation efforts, amplifying the call for responsible stewardship of our oceans.

  • Patronize responsible retailers and restaurants committed to sourcing sustainable seafood, thereby incentivizing ethical practices within the industry.

  • Educate yourself on various fishing methods and inquire about the origin, catch method, and handling of the fish you purchase, empowering yourself to make informed choices.

  • Engage with initiatives like the Sustainable Fish City campaign, galvanizing community action to promote sustainable seafood practices and foster a culture of environmental responsibility.

  • Reduce your overall consumption of fish and explore plant-based alternatives for protein, mitigating pressure on marine ecosystems while promoting personal health and well-being.

  • Participate in beach clean-up efforts and other conservation initiatives aimed at preserving marine habitats and safeguarding the delicate balance of marine ecosystems.

  • Advocate for policies and regulations that prioritize sustainable fishing practices and marine conservation efforts at local, national, and international levels, driving systemic change towards a more sustainable future for our oceans.
